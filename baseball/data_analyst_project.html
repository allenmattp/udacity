Baseball!¶

This project seeks to explore whether the Relative Age Effect (RAE) is present in major league baseball. We will examine the relationship (if any) between the month of year a player is born and their success as a baseball player.

Success as a baseball player will be measured through three different criteria:

  • Salary
  • Batting Average (Hits / At Bats)
  • Earned Run Average (Earned Runs allowed per game (27 outs))

Data used is from SeanLahman's Baseball Archive and includes playing statistics through the end of the 2016 season. Although there are advanced statistics that may better represent a player's proficiency, they are not included in this dataset and so are excluded.

Data Wrangling¶

In order to analyze the data, we need to organize it first. The following code calculates career statistics for us and saves them into dataframes. For both batting and pitching stats, an arbitrary cut-off needed to be set in order to avoid situations where a player finished their season hitting 1.000 due to a single lucky at bat, or a pitcher who had an infinitely large ERA because they failed to record an out.

Most major league records require a minimum of 60 games be played before a player's stats are eligible for consideration. The requirements for this project are in the same spirit but less stringent: for batters, they must have appeared at the plate at least 60 times in a season for their average to be included. Pitchers needed to record at least 60 outs for inclusion in the data. These number eliminated the outliers without whittling the players too much. Unlike major league records which seek only to track the best, because we want to evaluate players both above and below average we're interested more in including as many players as possible.

Note that salary data was not included for years preceding 1985. All players with data included, regardless of playing time, are included. The minimum amount a player earned during their career was \$60,000; to make plotting salaries easier they have all been divided by 10,000. The most a player earned over their career is \$398,416,252. I thought this was a mistake at first, but then I looked up Alex Rodriguez (b. July). Wow. These numbers aren't even adjusted for inflation.

Let's take a look at the distribution of batting averages. We would expect this to have a normal distribution with the bulk of batters being about average (around .250), with poor batters trending toward .200 (and lower) and the exceptional batters flirting with .300.

Observing the chart below we can see this is correct. As expected, the majority of hitters are average. Additionally, the distributino is left-skewed demonstrating that good hitters are harder to find than bad hitters!

Data Exploration¶

We've isolated the important stats for each category and observed that their distributions make logical sense. Let's now start to explore our initial question of whether the Relative Age Effect (RAE) is present in MLB players. We'll begin by exploring the first requirement to being a successful baseball players: Making it to the big leagues.

When we plot the distribution of players born each month, if we expect the RAE to have any significant bearing we should anticipate that some months will have significantly more players than others.

Our dataset shows that between 1366 (June) and 1831 (August) players were born during each month (on average 1567 players per month). An initial glance at the distribution may lead one to draw conclusions: August-born babies having a better chance of making the big leagues! Late-Summer to Early-Winter babies are better than Spring babies! Maybe there is something to the RAE!

However, before we get too far ahead of ourselves, it is important to recognize that the general population is not uniformly distributed. In fact, according to the CDC, August is regularly the month in which most babies are born. Consequently, it's likely not that a specific birth month provides inherent advantages to aspiring ball players but rather there are simply more babies born in that months than others, and thus a larger population to draw players from. A deeper statistical analysis (beyond the scope of this project) would be necessary to determine if these findings hold any significance.

Conclusions¶

Observing player AVG, ERA and Salary by month no obvious patterns emerge.

For batting average, the difference between the minimum (0.233251) and the maximum (0.237538) is less than half a percent. Over the course of 1000 ABs, the average player from the "best" month might have 4 more hits than the player from the worst month. The standard deviation between months is about 1 hit over the course of 1000 at bats.

Pitching ERA is a similar story. The worst month for pitchers sees a 4.339344 ERA; the best 4.195500. That's a little more than 1 ER difference every 10 games. The standard deviation between months is 0.048251; that's less than 1 ER over a period of 20 games.

Salary is a little more interesting, but as we saw with the distribution the data both has a smaller sample and more extreme outliers.

Interestingly, there doesn't appear to be a major relationship between batting AVG/ERA and salary either. This could be a result of there being signicantly less salary data (and the recent inflation of salaries). There does appear to be a positive relationship (albeit small) between AVG and salary and a negative relationship between ERA and salary (pitchers who allow fewer runs get paid more), which both makes sense. These relationships may warrant further examination!

While this is a cursory glance at the data making it difficult to draw any real conclusions regarding the presence of RAE in MLB, initial findings do not appear encouraging. Additional research could be undertaken to further explore the question:

  • Different stats could be analyzed
  • Specific time periods could be examined
  • More rigourous statistical analysis on findings
